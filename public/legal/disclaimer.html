8. Backtesting Is Hypothetical

The backtester feature allows you to explore how historical setups would have played out under simplified assumptions. These results are hypothetical and retrospective. They have important limitations:

  • Slippage: Backtests assume fills at exact prices. Real trades rarely fill at the exact price shown, especially for smaller or less liquid stocks.
  • Trading fees and commissions: Backtests do not account for brokerage commissions, options contract fees, or exchange fees.
  • Taxes: Backtests do not account for tax consequences of trading, including short-term vs. long-term capital gains treatment.
  • Order fills: In real markets, large orders move prices. A backtest does not model market impact.
  • Survivorship bias: The curated ticker list reflects currently active equities. Backtests may not account for companies that were delisted, went bankrupt, or were acquired during the backtest period.
  • Look-ahead bias: Despite our best efforts, backtesting systems can inadvertently incorporate future information. Treat all backtest results with healthy skepticism.

Hypothetical performance results have many inherent limitations. No representation is made that any account will or is likely to achieve results similar to those shown in backtests.

10. How to Read Scanner Output

This section explains how to interpret what the scanner shows you — and how not to interpret it.

What a "setup" is

A setup is a detected confluence of technical conditions on a stock's chart — things like price consolidating near a key level, volume contracting before a potential expansion, or a breakout of a defined pattern. The scanner identifies these conditions algorithmically based on price and volume data.

A setup is a research signal to investigate. Think of it as the scanner saying: "Something technically interesting may be happening here — worth a closer look." That's it.

What the scanner shows Don't read it as Read it as
A setup detection on a ticker A buy or sell signal A pattern worth investigating further
A reference price level (e.g., "near $206") An entry price to use A technical reference point the pattern formed around — confirm with your own analysis
A "stop" price level A guaranteed loss limit or a recommended stop The technical invalidation level for the pattern — where the pattern logic breaks down
A "target" price level A price the stock will reach A measured technical projection if the setup plays out — not a prediction
A "Breakout" or "Momentum" label A recommendation to buy A category label for the technical pattern type — for classification only
Backtester results Proof that a strategy works Historical context under simplified assumptions — see Section 8
An alert notification A trade instruction A heads-up that a condition you wanted to monitor has been met — still requires your own decision

The right research workflow

  1. Scanner flags a setup on a ticker.
  2. You open the ticker's chart in your own charting tool and verify the pattern independently.
  3. You research the company's fundamentals, upcoming catalysts, and sector context.
  4. You assess whether the potential trade fits your risk tolerance and account size.
  5. You decide — based on your own judgment — whether to do anything at all.

The scanner handles step 1 efficiently. Steps 2–5 are entirely yours.
